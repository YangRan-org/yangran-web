Dr. Yang teaches the courses where physics leaves the textbook. In every class, students don’t just learn theory — they design, build, break, debug, and ship working instruments as teams, developing the hard technical skills and the soft collaborative skills that define real-world engineering careers.

Experiential learning

Every lab is a design challenge, not a recipe. Students choose components, architect circuits, write firmware, test under real conditions, and present results. They learn by doing — and by failing, debugging, and iterating until the instrument works.

Hard skills meet soft skills

Teams rotate roles each week — Circuit Architect, Resource Manager, Hardware Specialist, Software Engineer, QA Lead. This mirrors real industry teams. Technical mastery alone is not enough; communication, delegation, risk management, and documentation are graded with equal weight.

PHYS 351

Advanced Scientific Instrumentation

Building on PHYS 252, this course takes students into the domain of real measurement — Raspberry Pi microprocessors, communication protocols (UART, SPI, I²C), sensor integration, motor control, and Python-driven data acquisition. Eight weeks of progressive labs lead to a final group project where everything converges into a single working instrument.

Final Projects ↓ Weekly Labs ↓
⚙

How students experience PHYS 351: Every Monday, teams gather in Small 230 for hands-on build sessions. Each student rotates through roles — Circuit Architect, Firmware Lead, QA Specialist — so nobody hides behind a single skill. Weekly peer evaluations keep accountability high. Progress is documented through photos, video clips, Git commits, and written reports with named task owners. By semester’s end, each team has produced not just a working instrument, but a professional user’s manual, a live demo, and a technical presentation — the complete experience of bringing a product from concept to delivery.

Final Projects · 2021–2025

Five years. Five cultures.
One question: what can an instrument do for the human heart?

Each fall, Dr. Yang designs a themed final project drawing from a different culture — Italy, Japan, the Netherlands, Tibet — broadening perspective as part of a holistic undergraduate education. She provides the concept, the technical specifications, and the philosophical framing. Teams of two to three students then spend approximately three months designing, building, and documenting their own unique interpretation, bringing the same theme to life in ways that are always different and always their own.

2025
🇨🇳 Cultural Theme — Tibet / China

Mindfulness Wheel

What turns here is not a plea; it is attention itself, returning to where it begins.

The most philosophically integrated project to date. Inspired by the Tibetan Zhuǎn Jīng Lún and the Mahāyāna tradition of returning attention to the heart, students built a secular, dual-rotor instrument that listens for a human cue — a spoken phrase, a clap cadence, a breath pattern, a gesture — and transforms it into balanced, meditative motion. Features extracted from the input deterministically seed rotation direction, speed profiles, and counts for both rotors. At session’s end, the same seed selects one of at least six mindfulness-aligned affirmations. Fully self-powered.

Dual Concentric Rotors Creative Input Deterministic Seed Affirmations Self-Contained Power E-Stop CSV Logging

Teams of 3 · ~3 months · Each team chose their own target user group, input modality, and affirmation medium

🇳🇱 Cultural Theme — The Netherlands

SolarZaan

Dutch heritage touches the sun.

Named for the windmills along the Zaan River at Zaanse Schans, this project challenged students to engineer a modern interpretation of a traditional Dutch windmill — powered entirely by solar energy. Each team built bidirectional blade rotation, variable speed control, and a translational mechanism (grinding, pulling, or alternative energy transformation), functioning under both artificial and natural light with full software control, emergency stop, and status monitoring.

Solar Powered Bidirectional Blades Translational Mechanism Voltage Regulation GUI / CLI E-Stop

7 teams of 3 · ~3 months · Unique blade assemblies, grinding mechanisms, decorative finishes

2023
🇯🇵 Cultural Theme — Japan

KaleidoMneme

Memory in light. Symmetry in motion.

Inspired by the Japanese aesthetic of beauty in transient patterns, KaleidoMneme challenged teams to build a Raspberry Pi–controlled kaleidoscope with at least three motor-driven rotating mirrors, multi-colored LED light sources, and an integrated Pi camera capturing 20-second video clips and still snapshots. A user interface let viewers pause the rotation and photograph the exact moment of beauty they wished to keep.

Raspberry Pi 3+ Rotating Mirrors RGB LEDs Pi Camera Motor Control UI Interaction

Teams of 3 · ~3 months · Each team chose their own mirror geometry, light palette, and enclosure

PHYS 252

Electronics

The foundation. Required for every physics major, this is the very first electrical engineering course most students will ever take — typically in sophomore year. Op-amps, filters, transistors, comparators, oscillators, and analog signal chains — designed, simulated in Multisim, soldered, and debugged from first principles on a breadboard. Nine progressive labs build fluency in analog electronics. Then, in the final two weeks, comes the real test: can you design something that works, entirely on your own?

Final Projects ↓
⚡

How students experience PHYS 252: Working in pairs during Week 1, students design their circuit on paper, simulate in Multisim, and submit a schematic with a bill of materials. In Week 2, they build alone — nobody may touch their circuit but them. No help from peers or TAs on debugging. They have three hours and an oscilloscope. If it works, an instructor signs off. If it doesn’t, they learn something more valuable: how to face a problem that has no answer key. This deliberate move from collaborative design to independent construction is the first step in Dr. Yang’s pipeline — teaching students to trust their own hands before they learn to trust a team.

Final Projects · 2022–2025

Four years. Four instruments.
One question: what human problem can this circuit solve?

Every spring, Dr. Yang designs a new final project that requires students to synthesize everything from the semester — op-amp topologies, filter design, signal conditioning, comparators — into a single working circuit. The project changes every year. The constraint never does: it must be built on one breadboard, with parts from the course inventory, in a single afternoon.

Spring 2025

MixSense

A stereo audio mixer combining two sources — background music and vocal track — with independent volume control, 2-band active tone shaping (bass at 100 Hz, treble at 10 kHz), and three logic-driven LED indicators showing channel presence and active mixing state.

Stereo Mixer Tone Control LED Logic LM358P ±12 dB
Spring 2024

SonicGlow

An audio volume visualizer driving 12+ LEDs across four color-coded threshold levels. Compatible with function generator, microphone, and 3.5mm audio jack. Features adjustable sensitivity, preamplifier with variable gain, and a low-pass filter for noise reduction.

LED VU Meter Comparator Preamp 3 Input Sources
Spring 2023

Audio Equalizer

A multi-band analog equalizer with at least three adjustable frequency bands (from sub-bass to upper treble), variable gain per band (±12 dB), and color-coded LED brightness indicators reflecting the gain level of each band in real time.

Multi-Band EQ Bandpass Filters LED Indicators ±12 dB Range
Spring 2022

Capacitance Meter

A C-to-V converter measuring capacitance from 10 nF to 100 nF using only discrete components — no oscilloscope, no function generator, no capacitance setting on the multimeter. Just analog ingenuity, a calibration curve, and five mystery capacitors to identify within 10%.

C-to-V Converter Calibration DC Only No Test Equipment

PHYS 471/472 & EPAD 495/496

Capstone EPAD & Honors Thesis

The summit. Students from Physics, Computer Science, Biology, and Neuroscience form interdisciplinary teams around a real problem — clinical, environmental, humanitarian — and spend a full year building something deployable. Honors thesis students (EPAD 495/496) go further: each one owns an individual deliverable within the team, conducts original research, and publicly defends their thesis before an interdisciplinary faculty committee to earn an Honors degree.

iRays Project ↗ Waveform.ai ↗ MCP Program ↗ Contact Dr. Yang
🎯

How students experience EPAD Capstone: This is where everything converges. Dr. Yang advises simultaneously on circuit design, measurement uncertainty, user research, and the ethics of what students are building. Teams write proposals, manage budgets, present at milestones, and deliver a working prototype with full documentation. Honors students go deeper still — defining an individual research question, reviewing literature, designing experiments, collecting and analyzing data, and defending their thesis before a faculty committee. Several capstone projects have become the seed of funded research, filed patents, and graduate-level work. Past advisees have gone on to programs at institutions including the University of Rochester. The yearlong format teaches project management, stakeholder communication, and the patience required to ship something that works in the real world.

Sensor-Augmented Acoustic Instruments

Where the performer’s body becomes the controller.

Traditional methods of integrating acoustic instruments into electronic music are half a century old and leave performers disconnected from the electroacoustic sound world they influence. Building on prior work with the Chaos­flöte and Melody Chua, this project develops a compact, multi-sensor device — measuring breath, embouchure, and motion — that attaches ergonomically to a range of instruments and feeds real-time data into digital audio workflows. The sequential engineering piece of Dr. Yang’s Waveform.ai program.

Electromagnetic Manipulation

Microwave Tweezers for Manipulation of Small Particles

Electromagnetic force as a precision instrument.

High-power microwave fields (6–18 GHz, up to 20 W) confined within narrow microstrip transmission lines create steep amplitude gradients capable of displacing and steering small dielectric spheres. Electromagnetic simulations (HFSS/FEKO) model the field; physical experiments test qualitative manipulation and routing along microstrip-defined paths — a potential gateway to microfluidic material transport.

Microwave 6–18 GHz Microstrip Design EM Simulation Microfluidics

Advisor: Prof. Seth Aubin (Physics)

Photonics & Nanofabrication

Particle Assembly Guided by Optical Tweezers

Pick, place, build — one microparticle at a time.

Advanced architected materials require structural features spanning nanometers to centimeters. This project builds a “pick and place” assembly system using focused laser beams (optical tweezers) to capture individual microparticles from suspension and position them precisely on a substrate. Surface engineering ensures adhesion; automation and coding bring repeatability.

Optical Tweezers Micro-Manipulation Surface Chemistry Automation

Advisors: Prof. Bjorg Larson (Physics) & Prof. Hannes C. Schniepp (Applied Science)

Materials & Cryogenics

Variable Temperature Probe for Magnetic Susceptibility

Measuring magnetism from 4 K to 400 K.

Design and construct a probe using AC magnetometry to measure the magnetic susceptibility of materials across a sweeping temperature range — from liquid helium to above room temperature. A small oscillating current generates an AC magnetic field; a pickup coil with lock-in detection measures the induced response. Precision mechanical and electromagnetic design for extreme conditions.

AC Magnetometry Cryogenic — 4 K Lock-In Detection Probe Design

Advisor: Prof. Mumtaz Qazilbash (Physics)
