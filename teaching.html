PHYS 351

Advanced Scientific Instrumentation

GPIB, SPI, I²C, lock-in detection, noise analysis, LabVIEW, Python DAQ. Students instrument real physical phenomena and must characterize not just the system, but the limits of what they can know.

  • 01

    Lock-In Amplifier: Detecting a Signal at −80 dBV

    [ Student Name(s) ] · Spring 20XX

    Software lock-in using a function generator reference and Python DAQ board. Demonstrated recovery of a 10 μV sinusoid buried in >100 mV broadband noise. Full noise floor and phase characterization.

    High Sensitivity Lock-In Python / DAQ
  • 02

    Pupil Diameter Tracking with Near-IR Camera

    [ Student Name(s) ] · Fall 20XX

    Sub-millimeter pupil tracking using a Raspberry Pi camera, custom 850 nm IR illumination ring, and OpenCV circle detection. Directly inspired by the iRays project. Validated under controlled luminance steps.

    iRays Adjacent Computer Vision IR Imaging
  • 03

    Automated Optical Thin Film Characterization

    [ Student Name(s) ] · Fall 20XX

    GPIB-controlled spectrometer and motorized sample stage automated via Python. Extracted refractive index dispersion curves and film thickness from interference fringes.

    GPIB Thin Films Optics
  • 04

    Environmental Radon Monitor, PIN Diode Array

    [ Student Name(s) ] · Spring 20XX

    Shielded charge-sensitive preamplifier driving a multichannel analyzer to count alpha events from radon decay. Measurements across campus over 72-hour windows with full statistical analysis.

    Radiation Detection Environmental
  • 05

    Scanning Tunneling Microscopy: Building the Piezo Controller

    [ Student Name(s) ] · Spring 20XX

    High-voltage piezo driver (0–150 V), coarse approach stepper, tunneling current I-V via transimpedance amplifier. Achieved atomic step resolution on HOPG graphite.

    Nanoscale High Voltage Surface Physics

PHYS 471/472 · EPAD Capstone

Students from Physics, CS, Biology, and Neuroscience form teams around a real problem — clinical, environmental, humanitarian — and spend a full year building something deployable. Dr. Yang advises simultaneously on circuit design, measurement uncertainty, user research, and the ethics of what they are building. Several capstone projects have seeded funded research and filed patents.

"The instrument is not the point. The question the instrument was built to answer — that is the point."
Dr. Ran Yang · PHYS 351, Opening Lecture
