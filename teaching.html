PHYS 351

Advanced Scientific Instrumentation

Building on the circuit foundations of PHYS 252, this course takes students into the domain of real measurement — Raspberry Pi microprocessors, communication protocols (UART, SPI, I²C), sensor integration, motor control, and Python-driven data acquisition. Eight weeks of progressive labs lead to a final group project where everything converges into a single working instrument.

Final Projects · 2021–2025

Five years. Five cultures.
One question: what can an instrument do for the human heart?

Each fall, Dr. Yang designs a themed final project that draws from a different culture — Italy, Japan, the Netherlands, and beyond — as part of a holistic approach to engineering education. She provides the concept, the technical specifications, and the philosophical framing. Teams of two to three students then spend approximately three months designing, building, and documenting their own unique interpretation — from circuit schematics to user manuals — bringing the same theme to life in ways that are always different and always their own.

2021
🇮🇹 Cultural Theme — Italy

Carousel

Where circuitry meets the piazza.

The inaugural themed project asked students to build a motorized carousel — a miniature Italian piazza attraction controlled by Raspberry Pi, with variable speed, directional control, and sensor-driven interaction. Teams designed their own structural forms, lighting sequences, and mechanical linkages, merging the romance of Italian craftsmanship with precise embedded-systems engineering.

Raspberry Pi DC Motor PWM Control LED Lighting Sensor Integration

Teams of 3 · ~3 months build time · Each team’s carousel was mechanically and aesthetically unique

2022
🇮🇹 Cultural Theme — Venice, Italy

Venice Gondola

Navigating the canals with code and current.

Building on the Italian motif, the second year sent students deeper — into the canals of Venice. Each team engineered a motorized gondola with bidirectional propulsion, steering control, and environmental sensing. The challenge was not just movement but grace: smooth acceleration curves, obstacle avoidance, and a user interface that felt as fluid as the waterways that inspired it.

Raspberry Pi Bidirectional Motor Steering Servo Distance Sensor GUI / CLI

Teams of 3 · ~3 months build time · Unique hull designs, propulsion systems, and navigation logic

2023
🇯🇵 Cultural Theme — Japan

KaleidoMneme

Memory in light. Symmetry in motion.

Inspired by the Japanese aesthetic of finding beauty in transient patterns, KaleidoMneme challenged teams to build a Raspberry Pi–controlled kaleidoscope with at least three motor-driven rotating mirrors, multi-colored LED light sources, and an integrated camera capable of capturing both 20-second video clips and still snapshots of the evolving patterns. A user interface allowed viewers to pause the rotation and photograph the exact moment of beauty they wished to keep.

Raspberry Pi 3+ Rotating Mirrors RGB LEDs Pi Camera Motor Control UI Interaction

Teams of 3 · ~3 months build time · Each team chose their own mirror geometry, light palette, and enclosure aesthetic

2024
🇳🇱 Cultural Theme — The Netherlands

SolarZaan

Dutch heritage touches the sun.

Named for the windmills along the Zaan River at Zaanse Schans, this project asked students to engineer a modern interpretation of a traditional Dutch windmill — powered entirely by solar energy. Each team built a windmill with bidirectional blade rotation, variable speed control, and a translational motion mechanism (grinding, pulling, or an alternative energy transformation). The system had to function under both artificial indoor lighting and natural daylight, with full software control including direction, speed adjustment, emergency stop, and status monitoring.

Solar Powered Bidirectional Blades Translational Mechanism Voltage Regulation GUI / CLI E-Stop

7 teams of 3 · ~3 months build time · Each team designed unique blade assemblies, grinding mechanisms, and decorative finishes

2025
🇨🇳 Cultural Theme — Tibet / China

Mindfulness Wheel

What turns here is not a plea; it is attention itself, returning to where it begins.

The most philosophically integrated project to date. Inspired by the Tibetan Zhuǎn Jīng Lún (prayer wheel) and the Mahāyāna tradition of returning attention to the heart, students built a secular, dual-rotor instrument that listens for a human cue — a spoken phrase, a clap cadence, a breath pattern, a gesture — and transforms it into balanced, meditative motion. Features extracted from the input deterministically seed rotation direction, speed profiles, and counts for both an inner and outer rotor. At session’s end, the same seed selects one of at least six genuinely supportive, mindfulness-aligned affirmations delivered through display, light, or sound. The system is fully self-powered — no lab bench supplies allowed.

Dual Concentric Rotors Creative Input Deterministic Seed Mapping Mindfulness Affirmations Self-Contained Power E-Stop CSV Logging

Teams of 3 · ~3 months build time · Each team defined their own target user group, input modality, and affirmation medium

∞

Across five years, the final project has evolved from a mechanical demonstration into a fully integrated design experience — encompassing power architecture, firmware state machines, user-centered interaction design, cultural research, and technical documentation delivered as professional user manuals. By 2025, every specification connects: the input modality shapes the motion, the motion shapes the message, and the message returns to the human who began it. The instrument has become coherent.

PHYS 471/472 · EPAD Capstone

Students from Physics, CS, Biology, and Neuroscience form teams around a real problem — clinical, environmental, humanitarian — and spend a full year building something deployable. Dr. Yang advises simultaneously on circuit design, measurement uncertainty, user research, and the ethics of what they are building. Several capstone projects have seeded funded research and filed patents.
