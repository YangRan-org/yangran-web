What students
build in the lab.

Dr. Yang teaches the courses where physics leaves the textbook. Students design, build, break, debug, and ship working instruments — developing the hard technical skills and collaborative instincts that define engineering careers.

The Pipeline

A four-course progression: from a sophomore who has never touched a breadboard to a senior leading a year-long product development cycle.

Sophomore

PHYS 252

Electronics

First EE course. Design in pairs, build alone. One breadboard, two weeks, one working circuit. Learn to trust your own hands.

 Junior

PHYS 351

Advanced Instrumentation

Teams of three, three months. Raspberry Pi, sensors, motors, firmware, cultural research, professional documentation. Build a complete instrument.

 Senior

PHYS 471/472

Capstone Projects

Interdisciplinary teams, one full year. A real problem, a real stakeholder, a deployable prototype. Budget, ethics, milestones, defense.

 Senior / Post

EPAD 495/496

Honors Thesis

One student, one question, one year. Original research contributing to funded programs. The closest thing to graduate work and high pay industry jobs an undergraduate can do.

Each stage builds on the last. Nothing is repeated.
Nothing is skipped. The skills compound.

Dr. Yang doesn’t just believe in experiential learning — she has engineered a concrete, multi-course progression that takes a sophomore who has never touched a breadboard and, over three years, transforms them into an engineer capable of leading a year-long product development cycle.
